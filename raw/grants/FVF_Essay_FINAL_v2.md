# Farmer Veteran Fellowship Fund Essay
## How Military Service Has Helped Me in Farming

I did not choose farming as a hobby or a lifestyle trend. I chose it because it made sense after a lifetime of service.

After years in the military, I was conditioned to operate with purpose. Missions mattered. People depended on each other. You planned, executed, adapted, and kept moving—especially when things went sideways. When I transitioned back into civilian life between deployments and duty cycles, I saw too many fellow veterans lose that sense of mission. Without structure, accountability, or a reason to get up before dawn, some struggled to find their footing again. I felt that pull myself.

Farming gave me something familiar: responsibility that doesn't wait, doesn't care about excuses, and doesn't stop because you're tired. Animals need to be fed. Systems need maintenance. Weather changes the plan. That environment mirrors military life in the best ways. Mission planning becomes crop rotations and breeding schedules. Discipline shows up at 0500, regardless of how you feel. Adaptability is required daily—because nature never follows the script. Leadership and resource management are constant, and quitting is not an option.

This is not retirement from service. It is reassignment.

I chose agriculture because it provides tangible results. You can see progress. You can see failure. You can fix things with your hands. For veterans who are used to measurable outcomes, that matters. Farming restores a sense of control and usefulness that many lose after taking off the uniform.

My focus on heritage livestock is deliberate. Conservation is a mission worth fighting for. These animals represent irreplaceable genetic lines that industrial agriculture has abandoned in favor of efficiency and uniformity. Once they're gone, they're gone forever. Arapawa goats, for example, have fewer than 300 registered animals in the United States. Preserving them is not nostalgia—it is safeguarding biodiversity, resilience, and agricultural options for the future.

That mission resonates deeply with me and with the veterans I work alongside. Many of us understand what it means to protect something vulnerable, overlooked, and worth saving.

The farm has also become a therapeutic space, whether we label it that way or not. I have watched veterans who struggle in classrooms or offices thrive when given responsibility over animals and land. Animals don't care about your rank, your past, or your worst day. They need you present and accountable right now. That grounding effect is powerful. It pulls people out of their heads and back into the moment.

This work is bigger than me. It is about building systems that last, preserving genetic heritage, and creating a place where veterans can find purpose again through meaningful labor. I farm because it aligns with how I was trained to live: with discipline, service, and responsibility. And because some missions don't end when you leave the military—they just change terrain.
