**PROJECT DESCRIPTION NARRATIVE (500 words max):**

Urban Ark Conservation is a veteran-owned 501(c)(3) nonprofit dedicated 
to preserving endangered heritage livestock breeds while providing 
agricultural therapy for veterans and first responders. Our 20-acre 
regenerative farm in rural Pennsylvania maintains breeding populations 
of critically endangered Arapawa goats, Highland cattle, and heritage 
poultry.

As a two-person operation led by an active-duty First Sergeant, we face 
significant infrastructure challenges. Our current feed storage is 
exposed to weather and pests, resulting in waste and inconsistent 
nutrition for our conservation animals. Several pregnant does require 
reliable shelter during rotational grazing, and damaged barn doors 
create drafts and predator vulnerability.

This project will install essential infrastructure to improve animal 
welfare and farm sustainability:

A 3-ton bulk grain bin will protect our feed investment from moisture, 
mold, and rodents. Currently, we lose approximately 10-15% of purchased 
feed to spoilage. Proper storage ensures our heritage breeds receive 
consistent, high-quality nutrition year-round.

A portable run-in shelter will provide weather protection during 
rotational grazing. Our Arapawa goats and Highland cattle move through 
paddocks for pasture recovery, but lack shelter access in outer 
paddocks. This is especially critical for pregnant does and young kids 
during spring kidding season.

Barn door repairs will eliminate drafts and improve predator protection. 
Our current doors are damaged, creating gaps that allow cold air and 
potential predator access. Secure nighttime housing is essential for 
our breeding animals.

A solar-powered livestock waterer will provide freeze-proof water 
access during Pennsylvania winters. Currently, we manually break ice 
and carry water multiple times daily in freezing temperatures - 
unsustainable for a two-person operation.

Additional electric fencing materials will improve our rotational 
grazing system, allowing more precise paddock divisions and better 
pasture management.

This infrastructure directly supports our conservation mission. 
Healthy, well-fed breeding animals produce healthy offspring, 
expanding endangered populations. Improved facilities also enable 
our veteran therapy programs - participants work alongside animals 
in safe, functional environments.

The project demonstrates how small farms can achieve sustainability 
through smart infrastructure investment rather than scale. Our model 
is replicable for other beginning farmers and veteran operations 
seeking to practice regenerative agriculture with limited resources.
