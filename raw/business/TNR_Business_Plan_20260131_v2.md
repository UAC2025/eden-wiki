## 2. BUSINESS DESCRIPTION

Terra Nova Roots LLC operates as the commercial arm of our conservation mission, working in partnership with Urban Ark Conservation Inc., the 501(c)(3) public charity I've led as Executive Director since 2016. This dual structure allows us to maximize both mission impact and financial sustainability—the LLC generates revenue through livestock and product sales, while the nonprofit delivers educational and therapeutic programming.

Our primary focus centers on heritage breed conservation, specifically our Arapawa goat herd. These remarkable animals, originally from New Zealand's Arapawa Island, represent one of the world's most endangered domestic goat breeds. With only 295 registered animals in the United States, every breeding decision carries significant genetic importance. Our herd of 15 animals (8 registered, 7 unregistered) represents approximately 3% of the entire U.S. population, making our breeding program critical to the breed's survival.

Heritage breeds matter because they carry genetic diversity developed over centuries to thrive in specific environments and conditions. As climate change and industrial agriculture present new challenges, these adapted genetics become increasingly valuable. Unlike commercial breeds selected for single traits like rapid growth or maximum milk production, heritage breeds offer resilience, hardiness, and unique characteristics that may prove essential for future food security.

My military background in counterdrug ground reconnaissance translates directly to the detailed observation and systematic approach required for effective breeding programs. The same skills I used to identify patterns and assess situations in combat environments now help me evaluate breeding decisions, monitor animal health, and optimize farm operations.

The agricultural therapy component emerged from my personal experience with the healing power of farm work after deployments. Working with animals and land provides grounding, purpose, and connection that many veterans find transformative. By combining this therapeutic benefit with meaningful conservation work, we create experiences that heal individuals while serving a larger purpose of preserving our agricultural heritage.

## 3. PRODUCTS & SERVICES

Terra Nova Roots generates revenue through four primary channels, each supporting our conservation mission while building business sustainability:

**Breeding Stock Sales:** Our registered Arapawa goats command premium prices due to their extreme rarity. Breeding does sell for $800-1,500, while proven bucks bring $1,000-2,000. With Belle due to kid imminently and Blossom following February 4th, we anticipate 6-10 kids this season. Maintaining detailed breeding records and genetic tracking allows us to offer customers documented lineages essential for conservation breeding programs.

**Farm Products:** Our diversified product line includes fresh eggs from heritage poultry, seasonal vegetables from our 4-acre garden area, pure maple syrup from our 10x12 sugar shack, and artisan smoked meats processed in our 8x8 smokehouse. These products sell through our 12x20 farm stand and local farmers markets, creating steady cash flow while showcasing the productivity of heritage livestock systems.

**Agricultural Therapy Services:** Through partnership with Urban Ark Conservation, we provide structured agricultural therapy experiences for veterans and first responders. Participants engage in daily animal care, rotational grazing management, and seasonal farm tasks. While the nonprofit handles program delivery, Terra Nova Roots provides the operational platform and receives facility use compensation.

**Educational Workshops:** We conduct heritage breed education workshops, sustainable farming classes, and conservation breeding seminars. Topics include rotational grazing systems, heritage breed selection, and small-scale processing techniques. These programs generate direct revenue while building market awareness for our breeding stock and establishing Terra Nova Roots as a regional conservation authority.

Our 2-story barn provides essential infrastructure for all revenue streams: hay storage and livestock housing support breeding operations, the upstairs chick-rearing area enables poultry production, and the 5 downstairs stalls accommodate breeding stock and visiting animals for workshops. The 20x20 pavilion serves as an outdoor classroom and event space, while our two 10x10 seedling greenhouses extend our growing season for vegetable production.

## 4. MARKET ANALYSIS

Three converging market trends create strong demand for Terra Nova Roots' products and services:

**Heritage Breed Market:** The American Livestock Breeds Conservancy reports growing interest in heritage breeds as consumers become more conscious of genetic diversity and sustainable agriculture. Arapawa goats' extreme rarity creates a seller's market, with breeding stock prices reflecting both conservation value and limited availability. Regional breeders consistently report waiting lists for quality breeding animals, and our documented breeding program positions us to capture premium pricing.

**Veteran Agricultural Therapy Market:** With over 20 million veterans in the United States and increasing recognition of agriculture's therapeutic benefits, demand for farm-based veteran services continues growing. The Veterans Administration's expansion of complementary therapy coverage and growing research supporting agricultural therapy's effectiveness creates opportunities for fee-for-service programs that complement our livestock operations.

**Local Food Movement:** Bradford County's proximity to major metropolitan areas creates strong demand for locally-produced, sustainably-raised food products. Our emphasis on heritage breeds, transparent production methods, and veteran ownership resonates with consumers seeking authentic food sources with meaningful stories behind them.

The convergence of these markets allows Terra Nova Roots to serve multiple customer segments while maintaining operational efficiency. The same infrastructure supporting breeding operations enables therapy programming, and the same animals providing therapeutic interaction produce the breeding stock that generates our highest margins.

## 5. OPERATIONS PLAN

Our 20-acre farm operates as an integrated system where each component supports multiple business functions. The 8 acres of rotational grazing pasture accommodate our goats, cattle, and pigs while demonstrating sustainable land management practices to workshop participants and therapy program clients.

Daily operations follow military-influenced systematic routines: morning animal health checks, pasture rotation assessments, and facility maintenance occur on established schedules that ensure consistency and reliability. The 16x14 chicken coop and two chicken tractors house our heritage poultry flock, providing fresh eggs for farm stand sales while enabling participants in therapy programs to experience the satisfaction of collecting eggs and caring for smaller livestock.

Our breeding program operates on documented schedules tracking genetics, breeding dates, health records, and performance data. With Belle due to kid any day and Blossom following February 4th, kidding season represents both our highest-stress and highest-reward period. Proper facilities and systematic monitoring ensure healthy births and strong kids essential for conservation genetics and future revenue.

The barn's dual-level design maximizes operational efficiency: upstairs hay storage reduces handling time and weather exposure, while downstairs stalls provide flexible space for breeding groups, quarantine areas, and workshop demonstrations. The attached feedlot enables controlled feeding during harsh weather or breeding season management.

Processing and value-added production occur in dedicated facilities: the smokehouse produces artisan meats for direct sale, the sugar shack processes maple syrup during spring season, and the two seedling greenhouses enable year-round growing season extension. The garage serves as solar equipment storage, supporting our commitment to sustainable operations while reducing operational costs.

Seasonal workflows maximize efficiency and revenue: spring kidding and maple syrup production, summer pasture management and therapy programs, fall breeding and harvest activities, winter planning and equipment maintenance. This cyclical approach ensures consistent activity levels while aligning labor demands with natural agricultural rhythms.
