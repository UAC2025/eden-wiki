# Arapawa Goat

**Entity Type:** Animal Breed
**Species:** Capra hircus
**Conservation Status:** Critical (The Livestock Conservancy)

The **Arapawa Goat** is a critically endangered heritage goat breed originally found on Arapawa Island, New Zealand. They are a medium-sized breed known for their hardiness, distinctive genetics, and excellent brush-clearing abilities. 

## Overview
- **Weight:** 60–80 lbs (Does), up to 125 lbs (Bucks)
- **Uses:** Meat, dairy, land management (brush/weed clearing)
- **Temperament:** Alert, docile, non-aggressive, good mothers.
- **Physical Traits:** Long hair (black, brown, white), often with badger stripes on the face. Typically horned. 
(source: raw/animals/2026-06-07-arapawa-goat-breed-standard.md)

## Breed History
Arapawa goats are believed to have arrived in New Zealand with European explorers in the 1600s or via Captain James Cook's expeditions in 1777. DNA analysis conducted in 2007 by The Livestock Conservancy and the University of Cordoba proved them to be a genetically distinct breed, refuting older theories that they were merely feral "Old English" goats. They may have distant links to South African breeds (source: raw/animals/2026-06-07-arapawa-goat-breed-standard.md).

The breed was nearly eradicated in the 1970s by the New Zealand Forest Service. Conservation efforts begun in 1987 by Betty and Walt Rowe created a sanctuary and returned 40 goats to domestication, forming the foundation of the modern domesticated population. The breed remains exceptionally rare (source: raw/animals/2026-06-07-arapawa-goat-breed-standard.md).

## Management Notes
Arapawas are highly adaptable but require shelter from heavy rain. They are notorious escape artists and require robust fencing. They bond closely with their family groups and human keepers (source: raw/animals/2026-06-07-arapawa-goat-breed-standard.md).
