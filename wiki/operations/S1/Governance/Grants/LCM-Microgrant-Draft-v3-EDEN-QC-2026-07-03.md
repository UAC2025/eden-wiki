### Need Statement

By The Livestock Conservancy's own registry and Conservation Priority List data, the Arapawa goat is classified **Critical** — the highest-urgency tier, defined as fewer than 200 annual North American registrations and a global population under 2,000 — with fewer than 400 breeding animals in the United States. It is one of the rarest goats in the world: descended from a feral population isolated on a single island in New Zealand's Marlborough Sounds and prized as a living genetic time capsule of the old English goat stock that has otherwise vanished. In a breed this small, every breeding animal is a measurable fraction of the entire gene pool. The loss of a single bloodline narrows the genetic future of the breed worldwide.

Urban Ark Conservation stewards a working Arapawa breeding nucleus on a regenerative conservation farm in Bradford County, Pennsylvania. **Before submission, insert live herd counts from the herd database with an explicit source/as-of date** (for example: “as of [DATE], UAC records show [COUNT] registered does, [COUNT] herd sire(s), and [COUNT] 2026 kids”). That nucleus faces the conservation problem that quietly extinguishes rare breeds — a **single-sire genetic bottleneck.** Every one of those 9 kids, and every kid born next season, shares one paternal line. Bred forward, that single line drives the herd's coefficient of inbreeding (COI) steadily upward, eroding fertility, kid vigor, and long-term viability. A herd that breeds only within itself does not conserve a breed — it slowly consumes it.

The solution is clear: introduce a second, genetically unrelated Arapawa bloodline. But that requires the **infrastructure** to do it properly — dedicated quarantine facilities, biosecure isolation paddocks, and permanent housing that allows a conservation breeding center to manage multiple bloodlines independently without disease risk or uncontrolled breeding. UAC currently has quality general livestock facilities, but lacks the specialized infrastructure needed to operate as a multi-sire **conservation breeding center** — separate buck housing, quarantine isolation for incoming animals, and a handling system designed for deliberate genetic management rather than general herd maintenance. A microgrant to build this infrastructure converts UAC from a farm that happens to keep a rare breed into a dedicated conservation breeding facility capable of serving as a node in the national Arapawa recovery network.

### Project Plan — Detailed Plan for Use of Grant Funds

The grant builds the physical infrastructure UAC needs to operate as a multi-bloodline conservation breeding center for a Critical breed. The project has two phases: **infrastructure first, then the animal that fills it.**

**Phase A: Conservation Breeding Infrastructure (Dec 2026 – Mar 2027)**

Grant funds construct a dedicated **conservation breeding paddock system** with:

1. **Isolation-quarantine paddock.** A separate, fenced enclosure with its own shelter and water — positioned at sufficient distance from the main herd to meet biosecure quarantine protocols. This allows any incoming animal (buck or doe) to complete a full CAE/CL/Johne's health screening cycle before contact with the existing nucleus.
2. **Breeding paddock and shelter.** A second, permanent buck housing and breeding enclosure separate from the general herd pasture, giving the conservation program the ability to manage controlled pairings, rest animals between breeding rotations, and keep multiple bloodlines physically separate.
3. **Handling and working facilities.** Upgraded handling infrastructure — sorting pens, chute, and catch area — designed specifically for individual animal management rather than group herd moves. A conservation breeding center needs to identify, restrain, and examine individual animals regularly (pedigree verification, health checks, genetic sampling, hoof care); the current general-purpose livestock handling makes this labor-intensive and imprecise.

Construction is a combination of purchased materials (fencing, gates, roofing, waterers) and UAC's own labor — a standard farm-build model that stretches grant dollars. UAC contributes the land, site preparation, and skilled labor as its matching share (see Budget).

**Phase B: Acquiring the Second Bloodline (Feb – Apr 2027)**

With infrastructure in place, UAC acquires and fully integrates an unrelated registered Arapawa buck to end the single-sire bottleneck:

1. **Source and acquire** — work through the Arapawa breed registry's referral network to identify a registered buck whose pedigree shows no overlap with the current sire's lines, maximizing genetic distance.
2. **Health screening and quarantine** — full veterinary examination and disease screening (CAE, CL, Johne's) during the isolation-quarantine period in the new facility, protecting the existing Critical population.
3. **Registry and breed-association enrollment** — register the buck, enroll UAC as an active breed-association member, and record the pedigree in UAC's breeding-management system.
4. **Genetic documentation (COI baseline and projection)** — calculate projected COI for planned pairings, establishing a documented before/after baseline.
5. **Breeding integration** — breed the new sire to selected, genetically appropriate does in the 2027 season, producing a kid crop carrying a second independent paternal line.

### Project Goals and Methods

**Goal:** Establish UAC as a permanent conservation breeding center capable of managing multiple Arapawa bloodlines independently — ending the current single-sire bottleneck and creating the infrastructure for sustained genetic diversity management.

**Method:** Build the separation infrastructure (quarantine paddock, buck housing, handling facilities) that a multi-bloodline breeding program requires, then acquire and integrate an unrelated registered sire as the first operational use of that infrastructure.

**Measurable objectives:**
- Construct quarantine and breeding paddocks with shelter, fencing, and handling facilities by March 2027.
- Acquire, screen, register, and integrate one unrelated registered Arapawa buck by spring 2027.
- Reduce the herd's projected next-generation COI from an estimated **~0.16–0.18** (continued single-sire breeding) to an estimated **~0.04–0.06** for offspring of the new, unrelated sire.
- Produce a 2027 kid crop carrying a documented second paternal line, with pedigrees recorded in the breed registry.

**COI methodology and baseline.** Estimates are derived using Wright's coefficient-of-inbreeding method applied to available pedigree depth for the current herd. Under continued single-sire breeding, offspring of closely related dams and the existing sire trend toward COI ≈ 0.16–0.18. Introducing a sire with no pedigree overlap drops the COI of his offspring toward the population baseline (≈ 0.04–0.06), since the paternal and maternal contributions no longer share recent common ancestors. UAC will refine these figures once the specific buck's pedigree is in hand and document the actual before/after values as a project deliverable. (These are pedigree-based projections, presented as estimates with stated methodology, not measured genotypic values.)

### Impact — How the Project Will Impact the Breed and Other Producers

**Impact on the breed.** This project builds a permanent conservation breeding node for a Critical CPL breed — not a one-season fix but a facility that enables multi-bloodline management for the life of the program. The quarantine paddock means future genetic acquisitions (bucks or does from other conservators) can be safely integrated without risking the existing herd. The separate breeding paddock means multiple bloodlines can be managed simultaneously, opening the door to a structured rotation rather than a single-sire bottleneck. Over time, UAC's facility becomes a reliable source of genetically diverse, less-related breeding stock for other Arapawa conservators.

**Impact on other producers.** UAC will share its paddock designs, quarantine protocols, and COI management methodology with fellow producers — particularly other small-scale conservation farms facing the same infrastructure gap. Because both bloodlines will be documented in the national breed registry, UAC's herd becomes a more useful node in the broader Arapawa conservation network for genetic outcrosses. The facility itself serves as a model: it shows what a Critical-breed conservation center looks like on a microgrant budget, making it replicable.

**Representation of The Livestock Conservancy (named deliverable).** As a formal commitment of this grant, **UAC will represent The Livestock Conservancy and display its Arapawa goats at no fewer than three local agricultural events during the grant year**, presenting the breed's conservation story, the Conservation Priority List, the role of TLC in heritage-breed preservation, and the story of how a microgrant built the infrastructure that saved this bloodline — giving a Critical breed visible public advocacy in a region where it is otherwise unknown.

### Evaluation — How We Will Evaluate Success

Success is defined by concrete, documented outcomes:

1. **Conservation breeding infrastructure completed** — quarantine isolation paddock, buck housing/breeding paddock, and handling facilities built and operational. *Measure:* construction photos, materials receipts, site inspection.
2. **Second bloodline acquired and integrated** — one unrelated registered Arapawa buck health-verified (CAE/CL/Johne's clear), registered, quarantined in the new facility, and bred. *Measure:* registry/transfer paperwork, veterinary records, quarantine log.
3. **COI reduction documented** — recorded before/after COI: projected single-sire offspring COI (~0.16–0.18) versus projected new-sire offspring COI (~0.04–0.06), refined from the actual buck pedigree. *Measure:* pedigree-based COI calculation in UAC's breeding-management system.
4. **Second paternal line established** — a 2027 kid crop produced from the new sire, with pedigrees recorded. *Measure:* registered kid records.
5. **Breed-association engagement** — active membership and herd registration completed. *Measure:* membership confirmation.
6. **TLC representation delivered** — three or more agricultural events attended with Arapawa goats and TLC materials. *Measure:* dated log of events.

A final project report documenting these six measures will be submitted to The Livestock Conservancy at the close of the project period.

**Sustainability.** The infrastructure built with grant funds — paddocks, shelters, handling facilities — is permanent and serves every future conservation activity at no recurring cost. The buck's ongoing care (feed, husbandry, veterinary costs) is absorbed through UAC's existing operating budget, the same way UAC sustains its current herd. The new bloodline compounds in value each breeding season at no further cost to TLC, and the resulting less-related offspring can be placed with other conservation herds to extend the genetic benefit across the breed.
